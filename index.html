The landmarking of New York

An animated map of the city's historic-district boundaries — 141 mapped areas — and when each was declared.

What this shows

Each shape is a New York City historic district — an area the Landmarks Preservation Commission (LPC) has designated so that changes to its buildings are regulated. Districts light up on the map in the order they were declared, colored by the decade of designation. Hover a district for its name, year and size; click it to pin the panel and open its LPC designation report and Wikipedia page. This map covers only designated, currently active historic districts — not individual landmarks, scenic landmarks, interior landmarks, or areas that were merely proposed.

The count (why it isn't a round number)

This map draws the 141 distinct historic-district areas in the LPC's current mapping layer, declared between Nov. 23, 1965 (Brooklyn Heights, the first, about seven months after the Landmarks Law of April 1965) and Nov. 25, 2025. By borough: Manhattan 70, Brooklyn 42, Queens and The Bronx 13 each, Staten Island 3.

The LPC's own tally is a bit higher — it has cited roughly 150 historic districts (2024) and 159 "historic districts and extensions" (late 2025). The difference is a counting convention: the commission tallies each designation action separately, while its mapping layer folds many later extensions back into the parent district's boundary. So 141 is the number of separate shapes you can see and click here, not a claim about the LPC's official count. (Thirteen extensions do appear as their own shapes and dates where the layer keeps them separate.)

Acres landmarked

The running acreage is the summed land area of every district shown, computed from the boundary polygons (shoelace area on the source State Plane coordinates, converted at 43,560 sq ft per acre). It matches the LPC dataset's own shape_area field to within 0.01%. All 141 districts together cover ≈ 4,179 acres, about 6.5 square miles — roughly the size of Central Park eight times over. Extensions don't overlap their parent districts, so nothing is double-counted.

Sources

Boundaries, designation dates and district names come from the LPC Historic Districts dataset on NYC Open Data (dataset skyk-mpzq), filtered to records where status is "designated" and the boundary is current. The LPC's headline counts are from its own materials (via the commission's Wikipedia summary and 2025 designation announcements). "Designation report" links open the commission's official PDF for each district at s-media.nyc.gov; "Wikipedia" links run a title search. Base map tiles: OpenStreetMap contributors and CARTO.

Notes & caveats

The designation date shown is the day the commission voted to designate the area as drawn. Where a district was later extended and the map layer merged that extension into the parent boundary, the whole current shape carries the original designation year — so a few districts appear larger, and earlier, than a strict "as first designated" map would show. Boundary polygons are derived by the LPC primarily from Department of City Planning tax-lot data and are simplified slightly here for the web (coordinates rounded to five decimals). Some designations have complicated histories (amended, re-designated or overturned); this map reflects the boundary the LPC currently marks as active.

A note on AI: This map was built with the help of AI. The underlying data is straight from the city's Landmarks Preservation Commission, but the processing and code have not been exhaustively hand-audited. Check any figure against the source dataset before relying on it.
